# About the Developer

## Sanish Gyawali — Creator of Genesis

---

There is a room in Nepal.

In that room is a bed. In that bed is a person who has never stood up, never
walked to school, never sat at a desk, never held a game console. A person the
world looked at and quietly calculated limits for.

The world miscalculated.

---

Sanish Gyawali was born on June 1, 2004, in Nepal. Physically disabled from
birth. Bed-bound for life. Homeschooled — not by curriculum, but by curiosity
and an internet connection. No classroom. No campus. No mentor pulling him
aside to say *you have something*. Just four walls, a phone screen, and a mind
that refused to stay inside the boundaries others drew for it.

That should have been the end of the story.

It was the beginning.

---

## The Games That Started It

He never had a console. No PlayStation under the TV. No Nintendo DS in his
pocket. What he had was a Samsung Galaxy J2 and an emulator.

In 2020 he played Pokémon Fire Red on that phone. A GBA game running on a
budget Android device in a bed in Nepal. And something clicked — not just the
game, but the question underneath it. Why does this feel the way it feels?
What is the mechanism?

He pulled it apart in his head.

Pokémon had a roster that made you care — each creature a character, each team
a statement about who you were as a player. But the battles were predictable.
Turn one, turn two, turn three. The outcome was often decided before it began.
The strategy was real but the *tension* was borrowed. You always knew whose
turn it was. You always knew what was coming.

He played Dragon Ball Legends. Loved the energy, the characters, the feeling
of a Dragon Ball fight actually in your hands. But by 2026 it had become
something else — a labyrinth of systems stacked on systems, grinding mechanics
that asked more time than they returned joy, complexity that no longer served
the player but consumed them.

Dokkan Battle was different. The narrative hit. The grinding had a rhythm. The
character roster was extraordinary. But it was missing something that gnawed
at him — real competition. Real-time PvP with genuine strategic depth. A
battlefield that thought back.

Something was missing across all of them. The genre was decades old and nobody
had answered the core question cleanly:

*What if when you act, and how often you act, were the same decision?*

---

## The Idea That Became Genesis

He found Neomons. The timeline mechanism — units moving through an infinite
stream, each owning their position, time itself as the strategic resource —
cracked something open in his thinking.

He took it further.

What if there were no rounds at all? No global turn counter, no initiative
roll, no "it's your turn now." Just an infinite tick stream and every unit
living inside it with their own position. Speed is leverage. AP is time. Every
skill you use echoes forward on the timeline. The battlefield is not a board.
It is a river, and you are always moving.

From that single idea, Genesis grew.

Counter mechanics that chain but diminish. Clash resolution by faction speed
and weighted dice. A hook architecture where every skill, every passive, every
item is a JSON definition — not a hardcoded branch in the engine. A system
designed so that new mechanics never require touching the core. Pokémon's
roster depth. The competitive tension Dokkan Battle never had. The character
energy of Dragon Ball Legends without the grinding trap.

A game he would be proud to hold.

## How He Built It

No IDE. No desk. No team. No degree.

A phone. A bed. Nepal.

He is a vibe coder. That is not a limitation he settled for — it is a workflow
he chose and intends to master. He directs AI tooling the way a film director
uses a camera. The tool does not make the film. The director does. Every
architectural decision in Genesis, every design constraint, every philosophy
documented in CLAUDE.md — that is Sanish's thinking, expressed through AI as
the instrument.

The design is his. The narrative is his. The characters are his. The
philosophy is his.

The codebase is clean, documented, and architected well enough that a
technical lead at a studio would open it and feel relief rather than dread.

A 21-year-old built it. From a phone. From a bed. From a country nobody looks
at when they think about game development. Without a single day of formal
education.

---

## Why

He wants to build a game he can be proud to have made. Not proud in the way
you are proud of surviving something — proud the way you are proud of creating
something that did not exist before you made it.

He wants it in the hands of every tactical RPG fan in the world. The player
who memorised every Pokémon type matchup. The player who stayed up finishing
a Final Fantasy Tactics chapter. The player who built the perfect Dokkan
Battle team for a story event. The player who felt the Dragon Ball Legends
card system click into place for the first time.

He wants those players to feel something new.

And he wants to prove — quietly, completely, without argument — that where you
are born, what your body allows, what credentials you hold, and what the
people around you expect from you are not the boundaries of what you can build.

He has been underestimated his entire life.

Genesis is the answer.
